“What can I do for you, my dwarves?” he said.

“Kili at your service!” said the one. “And Fili!” added
the other; and they both swept off their blue hoods and
bowed.

“At yours and your family’s!” replied Bilbo, remem-
bering his manners this time.

“Dwalin and Balin here already, I see,” said Kili. “Let
us join the throng!”

“Throng!” thought Mr. Baggins. “I don’t like the sound
of that. I really must sit down for a minute and collect my wits, and have a drink.” He had only just had a sip—in the corner, while the four dwarves sat round the table, and talked about mines and gold and troubles with the goblins, and the depredations of dragons, and lots of other things which he did not understand, and did not want to, for
they sounded much too adventurous—when, ding-dong-
a-ling-dang, his bell rang again, as if some naughty little
hobbit-boy was trying to pull the handle off.

“Someone at the door!” he said, blinking.

“Some four, I should say by the sound,” said Fili.
“Besides, we saw them coming along behind us in the
distance.”
